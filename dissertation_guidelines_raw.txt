PREFACE
The following Guidelines have been prepared to help students with the preparation, formatting and submission of dissertations to their respective departments. A dissertation is one of the requirements that every student must accomplish in order to earn a degree at Exploits University. This is in addition to completing the required course curriculum. These guidelines, therefore, presents the format that students need to follow when preparing their dissertation. This edition presents general rules to be followed by students from all departments. As such, it should be put in mind that individual departments might require their students to add some features which may not appear in this edition. Those other features may come in on the basis of the nature of the specific areas of study that the students embark on. It should also be noted that these guidelines are not rigid and not intended to suppress students’ innovation and creativity. They are intended to overcome challenges of presentation by providing a standard answer to the question thereof. The guidelines also act as a starting point for the Supervisor to provide guideline to the student based on the evidential gap that exists within deferent fields of study.

1.2	WHAT IS A DISSERTATION
A dissertation is a lengthy, original research paper that is a requirement for a degree either undergraduate, postgraduate or doctoral student. It is a comprehensive and systematic investigation of a specific topic or research question, demonstrating a student's expertise and contribution to their field. A dissertation typically involves extensive research, data collection and analysis, and critical thinking. It is an opportunity for students to showcase their knowledge, skills, and abilities, and to make a meaningful contribution to their field of study. The dissertation process helps students develop research skills, critical thinking, and academic writing abilities. The point of this paper is to showcase your skills and capacity to conduct research in your chosen discipline, and present the results through an original piece of content that will provide value for the academic and scientific community. 
Before we get any further, let's clarify one main thing: what is a dissertation? This term is usually used to present the final result of independent work and research for an undergraduate program. The word "dissertation" originates from Latin "dissertare," meaning "to discuss" or "to examine," and Old French "dissertation," meaning "a discussion or treatise." A thesis, on the other hand, is crafted for the completion of a Master's degree. Dissertation is also the final project that PhD candidates present before gaining their doctoral degree.
A dissertation enables you to explore a topic in more depth than an assignment essay. As it is your piece of work you have to:
have an idea for an area of research,
identify the evidential problem the study is trying to solve
breakdown your study into objectives  
identify your research questions,
decide how to undertake the research,
analyse and write up the data critically,
Writing a dissertation is a good preparation for post-graduate work.
A dissertation should be:
Should be SMART, a piece of small-scale research which can be completed, start to finish, in the amount of time you would spend in and out of class 3 – 6 months.
Enjoyable to carry out,
Organized into chapters and with a contents page, references and bibliography,
Often, but not always, based on your own personal experience of learning
Often, linking theory with practice
There should be some empirical research in your dissertation. The empirical evidence be of less than 10 years of published making it modest in scale but first-hand research will "lift" the overall quality of your dissertation and enable you to conclude, perhaps in a very qualified way, that your findings support, contradict, or modify the literature on the problem you have investigated. If there is no empirical research planned for your dissertation, talk to your supervisor about it!

1.4	DISSERTATION STRUCTURE
A dissertation should be a sustained argument. This means that it should draw upon the results of your reading, thinking and information-gathering in such a way that it could persuade readers to accept your understanding of the topic. In other words, the main aim is to use a selection of concepts, theoretical ideas, observations, statistical findings and your own faculties of criticism and imagination in an attempt to reach defensible conclusions about a topic which interests, challenges or puzzles you.
A very typical format of a dissertation is the discussion of a larger problem or issue followed by a smaller-scale piece of empirical research (e. g, the larger issue is why fewer men than women enter higher education as adult students, and the smaller-scale piece of research explores the issue in one higher education institution; the larger issue is “mentoring” as a means of staff development and the smaller-scale research explores mentoring in a workplace). The small scale empirical research thus throws some light on the larger issue. The larger issue provides a framework within which the small scale research sits. Each section serves a distinct purpose, ensuring clarity and coherence. Section 1, Understanding Dissertation, outlines the research’s scope, objectives, and significance, setting the foundation for the study. It clarifies the research problem, defines key terms, and establishes the study’s relevance, guiding readers through the dissertation’s framework and expectations.

1.5.2.1	What is Research problem. 
A research problem is what the researcher is interested in finding out or studying. It can be defined as a statement about an area of concern, a condition to be improved, or a difficulty to be eliminated. However, (Madhu D et al., 2024) argue that  “In the research process, the initial and most crucial step is the selection and precise definition of a research problem”. A researcher must identify and articulate the Problem in a manner that makes it amenable to investigation. Similar to agricultural scientist, a researcher must carefully assess all symptoms associated with a problem of plants, whether presented to them or observed, before making an accurate diagnosis for it. To define a problem accurately, a researcher must understand its nature: what constitutes a problem?
1.5.2.2	What are the Sources of a research problem? 
Personal interest and experience. 
Your everyday experiences are a good source of research problem. You have to think critically about your personal experiences with an issue that affects your family, your personal life, or your community. A research problem derived from personal experience can spring from any issue and from anywhere. For example, you can construct a research problem from events that appear to be out of the ordinary or from community relationships that don’t have clear explanations.
Deductions from Theory
Inferences drawn by a researcher from the generalizations of life in a society they are familiar with are referred to as deductions from theory. After placing the deductions in an empirical framework and developing a hypothesis based on a theory, a researcher formulates a research topic and a hypothesis that proposes some conclusions based on the empirical data.

In order to determine whether a hypothesis accurately describes the state of an affair, the research takes the relationship into consideration. The following step is a methodical analysis that determines whether the actual data supports or refutes the hypothesis.
Interdisciplinary perspective
If you consider interdisciplinary perspective to identify a problem for a research study, you’ll have to look at scholarship and academic movements from outside your main area of investigation. It’s an intellectually involving process, one that requires reviewing pertinent literature to discover unique avenues of exploration an analysis. The benefit of using this approach to identify a research problem for your research paper assignment is that it presents an opportunity for you to understand complex issues with ease.
Use of intellectual curiosity. One can ask oneself: "How?" "Why?" and so on.  
Intellectual curiosity drives the identification of research problems by encouraging inquisitive thinking. Researchers often ask probing questions like "How?" or "Why?" to uncover gaps in knowledge or explore phenomena. This curiosity fosters critical examination, leading to innovative inquiries and the formulation of meaningful research topics. According to Gill and Gill (2021), intellectual curiosity is pivotal in initiating thoughtful exploration, challenging assumptions, and seeking solutions that expand understanding within a field. It forms the foundation of academic and scientific investigation
Relevant Literature
To generate a research problem from relevant literature, you first have to review research related to your area of interest. Doing so allows you to find gaps on the topic, making it easy for you to understand just how much understudied your area of interest is.
Data collected from relevant literature is relevant because it helps to:
Fill existing gaps in knowledge based on specific research
Determine if current studies can have implications on further research on the same issue
See if it’s possible to conduct a similar study in a different area or apply the same in a different context
Determine if the methods used in previous studies can be effective in solving future problems
We can’t stress enough on the value of existing literature. The results should point you towards an outstanding issue, give suggestion for future gaps, and make it possible to delineate gaps in existing knowledge.
Using prior research done by other researchers in case there are recommendations given for further studies. 
During evaluation of a specific program one can identify a gap which needs research.

1.5.3	 GETTING STARTED: WRITE IT DOWN
Once you have completed the Research Methods Course you and have presented your proposal, collected and finished analyzing it, start writing. Write it down! References, dates, articles you see in newspapers. You won't remember and it will take hours to chase up half-forgotten facts in six months’ time.
Keep in mind that you will write Chapters 1 through 3 as your dissertation proposal. However, there are changes that typically need to be made in these chapters to enrich the content or to improve the readability as you write the final dissertation. Often, after data analysis is complete, the first three chapters will need revisions to reflect a more in-depth understanding of the topic and to ensure consistency. To ensure the quality of both your proposal and your final dissertation and reduce the time you’re writing needs to reflect academic level, scholarly writing standards from your very first draft.
Each section within the proposal or dissertation should be well organized and easy for the reader to follow. Each paragraph should be short, clear, and focused. A paragraph should (1) be three to eight sentences in length, (2) focus on one point, topic, or argument, (3) include a topic sentence the defines the focus for the paragraph, and (4) include a transition sentence to the next paragraph. Include one space after each period. There should be no grammatical, punctuation, sentence structure, or referencing errors.
Verb tense is an important consideration for Chapters 1 through 3. For the proposal, the researcher uses future tense (e.g. “The purpose of this study is to…”), whereas in the dissertation, the chapters are revised to reflect past tense (e.g. “The purpose of this study was to…”). Taking the time to ensure high quality, scholarly writing for each draft will save you time in all the steps of the development and review phases of the dissertation process so make sure to do it right the first time!
1.5.4	GETTING STARTED: TIME-MANAGEMENT
The beginning of the final semester of your fourth years may seem a long way off now but it will come round quickly so start writing early on. See your supervisor frequently. Don't put off writing or making appointments. Writing a dissertation is one of the most challenging and time-consuming tasks in a graduate student’s academic journey. Balancing research, writing, and other responsibilities requires effective time management. Without a strategic approach, it’s easy to feel overwhelmed. Here’s how to master time management while writing your dissertation.
1. Start with a Detailed Plan
Before diving into the writing process, create a comprehensive plan. Break down the dissertation into manageable sections: introduction, literature review, methodology, data analysis, and conclusion. These sections correspond to the typical dissertation structure. Set realistic deadlines for each section, considering the time needed for research, writing, and revisions. A clear plan helps you visualize the entire process and reduces the likelihood of last-minute rushes.
Figure (Own Source, 2025) 

2. Prioritize Tasks
Not all tasks are equally important or time sensitive. Identify high-priority tasks that have the most significant impact on your progress, such as completing key sections of your dissertation or meeting with your advisor. Use tools like the Eisenhower Matrix to distinguish between urgent and important tasks. Focusing on these high-priority tasks ensures steady progress, preventing you from getting bogged down by less critical activities.
3. Create a Consistent Schedule
Consistency is key when working on a long-term project like a dissertation. Set aside dedicated time each day or week for dissertation work. Whether it’s early mornings, late evenings, or weekends, find a time that works best for you and stick to it. Consistent effort, even in short bursts, accumulates over time, leading to significant progress without the stress of cramming.
4. Avoid Procrastination
Procrastination is a common pitfall for dissertation writers. To combat this, break down tasks into smaller, manageable steps. For example, instead of setting a goal to “write the literature review,” aim to “draft an outline for the literature review” or “write the first paragraph.” Smaller tasks feel less daunting and are easier to start, which helps maintain momentum.
5. Use Tool and Technology
Leverage technology to stay organized and on track. Tools like Trello, Asana, or even simple to-do lists can help you manage tasks and deadlines. Time-tracking apps like Toggl can also provide insights into how much time you’re spending on different aspects of your dissertation, allowing you to adjust your schedule as needed. Use of Mendeley reference manager, R discovery, Lit map and other tools to hep in connecting your resource data.  
6. Allow for Flexibility
While sticking to a schedule is important, it’s equally important to allow for flexibility. Research can take unexpected turns, or life events might disrupt your planned timeline. Build buffer time into your schedule to accommodate these changes without derailing your progress.

2.1.2	DECLARATION
A Declaration in a dissertation or thesis is a formal statement in which the student affirms the originality of their work, acknowledges the contributions of others, and confirms compliance with academic integrity and ethical standards. It is typically a mandatory section included in the front matter of the thesis or dissertation.
Key Elements of a Declaration:
Originality Statement – Confirms that the work is the author's own and has not been plagiarized.
Acknowledgement of Sources – States that proper citations and references have been provided for all external sources.
Compliance with Academic Rules – Declares adherence to institutional guidelines on research ethics (e.g., no fabrication, falsification, or misconduct).
Previous Submission – If applicable, clarifies whether any part of the work has been submitted for another degree.
Collaboration &amp; Assistance – May acknowledge help from supervisors, peers, or funding bodies.

The Certificate of Approval (also called Thesis/Dissertation Approval Form) is a formal document included in a thesis or dissertation that confirms the work has been reviewed and accepted by the examining committee or advisors. It serves as official proof that the research meets the academic standards required by the institution.
Purpose of the Certificate of Approval:
Confirms that the thesis/dissertation has been examined and approved by the supervisory committee.

2.1.4	ACKNOWLEDGEMENTS
The Acknowledgements section is a personal part of your dissertation or thesis where you express gratitude to the individuals and organizations that supported your research journey. Unlike the rest of your academic work, this section allows for a more informal and heartfelt tone.
2.1.5	DEDICATION 
The Dedication is a brief, personal statement at the beginning of your thesis or dissertation where you honor individuals or groups who inspired or supported you. Unlike the Acknowledgements (which thanks contributors in detail), the dedication is usually short, emotional, and meaningful often just 1-2 sentences.
2.1.6	ABSTRACT
An abstract is a concise summary of your entire dissertation or thesis, typically 150 –300 words long. It provides readers with a quick overview of your research, including the purpose, methods, key findings, and significance of your work. It is usually the first section people read (after the title) and helps them decide whether to read the full document.
Key Elements of an Abstract
A well-written abstract should include:
Research Problem/Background (1-2 sentences)
What gap in knowledge does your study address?
Why is this research important?
Research Objectives/Questions (1-2 sentences)
What did you aim to discover or prove?
Methodology (2-3 sentences)
Briefly describe your research design (qualitative/quantitative), data sources, and analysis techniques.
Key Findings/Results (2-3 sentences)
Summarize the most important outcomes of your study.
Conclusion &amp; Implications (1-2 sentences)
What do your findings mean for the field?
Are there practical or theoretical contributions?
2.1.7	TABLE OF CONTENTS (Automated using Microsoft styles) 
A dissertation table of contents is a structured list of the chapters, sections, and subsections within the document, along with their corresponding page numbers. It serves as a roadmap, providing a clear overview of the dissertation's organization. This section is essential because it helps readers quickly locate specific topics, enhances document navigation, and establishes a professional and systematic presentation of the research.
An automated Table of Contents (TOC) in Microsoft Word dynamically updates page numbers and headings when changes are made. By using Heading Styles, you can generate a professional TOC with different formatting levels (e.g., Chapter headers in Size 14, subheadings in Size 12).
2.1.7.1	Step-by-Step Guide to Creating an Automated TOC
1. Apply Heading Styles to Your Document
Before generating a TOC, assign Heading Styles to your chapters and subheadings:
Chapter Titles (Level 1) → Heading 1 (Size 14, Bold)
Section Subheadings (Level 2) → Heading 2 (Size 12, Bold)
Sub-sections (Level 3) → Heading 3 (Size 12, Bold)
Further divisions (Levels 4-5) → Heading 4-5 (Size 12 Bold)
How to Apply Styles:
Select a chapter title (e.g., "Chapter 1: Introduction").
Go to the Home tab.
In the Styles group, click Heading 1.
Repeat for subheadings (use Heading 2, Heading 3, etc.).

2. Modify Heading Styles (Optional – Customize Font Sizes)
If the default styles don’t match your requirements (e.g., Chapter headers in 14pt, others in 12pt), modify them:
Right-click Heading 1 in the Styles panel.
Select Modify.

Change Font Size to 14, Bold, and adjust spacing if needed.
Click OK.
Repeat for Heading 2-5 (set to Size 12 with variations like bold/italic).

3. Insert the Automated Table of Contents
Place the cursor where the TOC should appear (usually after the Abstract).
Go to the References tab.
Click Table of Contents → Choose a preset (Automatic Table 1 or 2) or Custom Table of Contents.
In the pop-up window:
Set Show levels to 5 (if using Heading 1-5).
Adjust formatting (e.g., dotted lines, right-align page numbers).
Click OK to generate.

4. Update the TOC Automatically
If you edit headings or page numbers:
Click anywhere inside the TOC.
Click Update Table (top-left corner).
Choose:
Update page numbers only (if only numbering changed).

1.1	Introductions 1 – 2 pages
It’s fair to assume that because the abstract and introduction are the first chapters to be read by someone reading your dissertation, it means they must be written first also. But in reality, this isn’t the case. You’ll actually be far better off writing your dissertation introduction, conclusion and abstract after you have written all the other parts of the dissertation. The introduction must include sub-sections with appropriate headings/subheadings and should highlight some of the key references that you plan to use in the main study. This demonstrates another reason why writing a dissertation introduction last is beneficial. As you will have already written the literature review, the most prominent authors will already be evident and you can showcase this research to the best of your ability.
left31750Your Introduction sets the stage for your research, explaining what you’re studying, why it matters, and how you’ll approach it. Here’s how to structure it based on your guide:
1. When to Write the Introduction Write it after your Literature Review (so you understand existing research gaps). Finalize it before the Conclusion (to ensure alignment with your findings).
1.1.1 Introduce the Topic &amp; Background
Begin by providing a broad overview of your research topic before narrowing it down to your specific focus. For example: "Remote work has become a global trend, particularly in the tech industry. However, its impact on productivity within startup environments remains understudied." Clearly define key terms such as "remote work" and "productivity" to ensure readers understand the scope of your study. This helps establish context and prevents ambiguity.
State the Research Significance
Explain why your research matters and why readers should care about your study. For instance: "This study addresses a critical gap in the existing literature by focusing specifically on startups, offering practical insights that can help emerging businesses optimize their remote work policies."  This section should highlight the unique contribution of your research and its relevance to academia or industry.
Why Your Research Matters &amp; Necessity: What Problem Does It Solve?
Identify the gap or problem your research addresses. Example: "Previous studies have primarily examined remote work in large corporations, overlooking the unique challenges faced by startups, such as limited resources and dynamic team structures."
Urgency: Why Is This Research Important Now?
Highlight the timeliness of your study. Example: "Since the pandemic, over 70% of startups have adopted remote work, yet there is still limited understanding of how this shift impacts their productivity and team dynamics."
Significance: Who Benefits from This Research?
Explain the practical or theoretical implications of your findings. Example: "The results of this study will provide startup founders and HR managers with evidence-based strategies to enhance productivity while maintaining team cohesion in remote settings."
How to Structure the Flow of Your Introduction
1. Hook: Engage Your Reader
Start with a compelling question or statistic to capture attention. Example: "Can remote work sustain productivity in the fast-paced, high-pressure environment of a startup?"
2. Context: Review Existing Research and Limitations
Summarize key studies while pointing out their gaps. Example: "While Smith (2020) explored remote work efficiency in large tech firms, startup environments differ significantly due to their smaller teams, agile workflows, and rapid scaling demands."
3. Rationale: Explain Your Research Approach
Justify your methodology. Example: "This study employs a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative productivity metrics with qualitative employee feedback to provide a comprehensive analysis of remote work’s impact in startups."
4. Hypothesis/Objectives: Clearly State Your Research Goals
Present your hypothesis or research questions explicitly. Example: "We hypothesize that while remote work enhances individual productivity in startups, it may weaken team cohesion due to reduced face-to-face interactions."
1.2	Background to the study 2 – 3 pages
The "background to the study" in a dissertation or research paper is the introductory section that sets the context, provides a rationale for the research, and explains the significance of the study. It establishes the broader landscape of the research topic, showing why the study is necessary and how it fits within the existing body of knowledge.
Why is the Background of the Study Important?
No matter how surprising and important the findings of your study are, if you do not provide the reader with the necessary background information and context, they will not be able to understand your reasons for studying the specific problem you chose and why you think your study is relevant. And more importantly, an editor who does not share your enthusiasm for your work (because you did not fill them in on all the important details) will very probably not even consider your dissertation worthy of their and the reviewers’ time and will immediately send it back to you. 
To avoid such desk rejection, you need to make sure you pique the reader’s interest and help them understand the contribution of your work to the specific field you study, the more general research community, or the public. Introducing the study background is crucial to setting the scene for your readers.
Background of the Study Structure
Before writing your study background, it is essential to understand what to include. The following elements should all be included in the background and are presented in greater detail in the next section: 
A general overview of the topic and why it is important (overlaps with establishing the “importance of the topic” in the Introduction)
The current state of the research on the topic or on related topics in the field
Controversies about current knowledge or specific past studies that undergird your research methodology
Any claims or assumptions that have been made by researchers, institutions, or politicians that might need to be clarified
Methods and techniques used in the study or from which your study deviated in some way
Presenting the Study Background
As you begin introducing your background, you first need to provide a general overview and include the main issues concerning the topic. Depending on whether you do “basic” (with the aim of providing further knowledge) or “applied” research (to establish new techniques, processes, or products), this is either a literature at summarizes all relevant earlier studies in the field or a description of the process (e.g., vote counting) or practice (e.g., diagnosis of a specific disease) that you think is problematic or lacking and needs a solution. 
Examples of a general overview
If you study the function of a Drosophila gene, for example, you can explain to the reader why and for whom the study of fly genetics is relevant, what is already known and established, and where you see gaps in the existing literature. If you investigated how the way universities have transitioned into online teaching since the beginning of the Covid-19 pandemic has affected students’ learning progress, then you need to present a summary of what changes have happened around the world, what the effects of those changes have been so far, and where you see problems that need to be addressed. Note that you need to provide sources for every statement and every claim you make here, to establish a solid foundation of knowledge for your own study. 
Describing the current state of knowledge
When the reader understands the main issue(s), you need to fill them in more specifically on the current state of the field (in basic research) or the process/practice/product use you describe (in practical/applied research). Cite all relevant studies that have already reported on the Drosophila gene you are interested in, have failed to reveal certain functions of it, or have suggested that it might be involved in more processes than we know so far. Or list the reports from the education ministries of the countries you are interested in and highlight the data that shows the need for research into the effects of the Corona-19 pandemic on teaching and learning. 
Discussing controversies, claims, and assumptions
Are there controversies regarding your topic of interest that need to be mentioned and/or addressed? For example, if your research topic involves an issue that is politically hot, you can acknowledge this here. Have any earlier claims or assumptions been made, by other researchers, institutions, or politicians, that you think need to be clarified? 
Mentioning methodologies and approaches
While putting together these details, you also need to mention methodologies: What methods/techniques have been used so far to study what you studied and why are you going to either use the same or a different approach? Are any of the methods included in the literature review flawed in such a way that your study takes specific measures to correct or update? While you shouldn’t spend too much time here justifying your methods (this can be summarized briefly in the rationale of the study at the end of the Introduction and later in the Discussion section), you can engage with the crucial methods applied in previous studies here first. 
When you have established the background of the study of your research paper in such a logical way, then the reader should have had no problem following you from the more general information you introduced first to the specific details you added later. You can now easily lead over to the relevance of your research, explain how your work fits into the bigger picture, and specify the aims and objectives of your study. This latter part is usually considered the “Statement of the problem” of your study. Without a solid research paper background, this statement will come out of nowhere for the reader and very probably raise more questions than you were planning to answer.  
1.3	Statement of the problem 
 A research problem statement is a concise statement describing the problem or issue addressed by the research study. The research problem should be composed in a way that both experts and non-experts in the field can understand. Every research paper describes the investigation of a problem: by adding knowledge to the existing literature, revisiting known observations, or finding concrete solutions. What contribution your publication makes to your field or the scientific community at large depends on whether your research is “basic” (i.e., mainly interested in providing further knowledge that researchers can later apply to specific problems) or “applied” (i.e., developing new techniques, processes, and products).

In any case, a research proposal or research paper must clearly identify and describe the “problem” that is being investigated, so that the reader understands where the research comes from, why the study is relevant, if the applied methods are appropriate, and if the presented results are valid and answer the stated questions. This is known as the “statement of the problem.”
Understanding How to Write a Research Problem
A research problem statement is a concise description of the issue or problem that your research aims to address. It serves as the foundation for your entire research project by clearly articulating the focus of your study (Creswell &amp; Creswell, 2018). This acts as the basis of the problem formulation where it formulates the roots of the entire study. The research is a tree as presented in the introduction section. Therefore, let’s look at steps for formulating the problem statement of the study. Which originates for the literature we have gathered from Topic formulation, introduction and background to the study.    
Step 1: Provide Context for Your Problem
The first step in crafting a strong research problem statement involves establishing the background and context of your research problem. According to Booth et al. (2016), this contextual foundation helps readers understand the broader landscape in which your research problem exists. When providing context, you should address "what do we already know?" about the topic. This contextual framing should include relevant existing knowledge, historical background, and theoretical frameworks that inform your understanding of the problem (Maxwell, 2013). For example, if studying digital literacy among older adults, you might provide context about current technology adoption rates, existing digital divide literature, and demographic trends.
Step 2: Describe the Exact Issue Your Research Will Address
The second step requires articulating the specific issue or problem your research will tackle. Bryman (2016) emphasizes that this section should clearly identify the knowledge gap or practical problem your research aims to fill. This is where you address "what do we still need to know?" This component should precisely delineate the boundaries of your research focus, avoiding overly broad or vague descriptions (Saunders et al., 2019). For instance, rather than stating "social media affects teenagers," you might specify "the relationship between Instagram usage and body image perception among female adolescents aged 13-17 in urban environments."
Step 3: Show the Relevance of the Problem
The third step involves establishing why your research problem matters. According to Kumar (2019), this section should answer the question "why do we need to know more about this?" by demonstrating the significance of your research problem. To establish relevance, you should articulate how addressing this problem contributes to advancing knowledge, resolving practical issues, or benefiting specific stakeholders (Punch, 2016). This might include discussing potential theoretical contributions, practical applications, or social implications. For example, research on medication adherence among chronically ill patients might emphasize the potential to improve health outcomes and reduce healthcare costs.
Step 4: Set Your Research Objectives
The final step involves clearly stating what your research aims to accomplish by addressing "what will you do to find out more?" As noted by Sekaran and Bougie (2016), well-defined research objectives provide direction for your methodology and analysis. Your objectives should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) (Bell et al., 2018). These objectives typically begin with action verbs like "identify," "analyze," "evaluate," or "determine" and should directly align with addressing your research problem. For example, "To examine the relationship between leadership styles and employee retention rates in technology startups founded between 2018-2023."
Characteristics of a Strong Problem Statement
As shown in the image, a strong research problem statement exhibits several key characteristics:
Specificity
According to Leedy and Ormrod (2019), a problem statement should focus on a single, well-defined issue rather than attempting to address multiple problems simultaneously. This focus ensures clarity and manageability of your research.
Conciseness
Walliman (2017) emphasizes that a problem statement should be succinct and precise, typically expressible in 1-3 sentences that capture the essence of your research problem. Unnecessary details should be eliminated to maintain focus.
Measurability
Finally, Neuman (2014) highlights the importance of ensuring your problem statement connects to measurable objectives. This means articulating how you will assess progress toward addressing your research problem, whether through quantitative metrics, qualitative assessments, or mixed methods approaches.
By carefully developing each of these components, you create a research problem statement that effectively communicates the focus, purpose, and significance of your research to academic and professional audiences.
Your research problem defines the gap in existing knowledge you want to address (e.g., global warming causes), an issue with a certain process (e.g., voter registration) or practices (e.g., patient treatment) that is known and well documented and needs a solution, or some surprising phenomena or earlier findings that point to the need for further investigation. Your approach can be theoretical or practical, and the specific type of problem you choose to address depends on the type of research you want to do. 
In any case, your paper should not repeat what other studies have already said. It also should not ask a question that is too broad in scope to be answered within your study, nor should it be so vague that your reader cannot grasp your motivation or focus. To avoid such problems, you need to clearly define your research question, put it into context, and emphasize its significance for your field of research, the wider research community, or even the general public.
1.4	Objectives of the Study  
Research objectives describe what your research is trying to achieve and explain why you are pursuing it. They summarize the approach and purpose of your project and help to focus your research. Research objectives form the backbone of any scholarly investigation, providing clear direction for what the study intends to accomplish (Creswell &amp; Poth, 2018). As indicated in academic literature, "Research objectives describe what your research project intends to accomplish. They should guide every step of the research process, including how you collect data, build your argument, and develop your conclusions" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 1).
Distinguishing Between Research Aims and Objectives
An important distinction exists between research aims and objectives that students must understand. According to Kumar (2019), research aims represent broad statements indicating the general purpose of your research project, while research objectives are more specific statements that detail the particular focus and approach of your study.
As noted by research methodologists, "A research aim typically refers to a broad statement indicating the general purpose of your research project. It should appear at the end of your problem statement, before your research objectives" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 1). While a research project typically has one overarching aim, it will contain multiple research objectives that operationalize how that aim will be achieved (Bryman, 2016).
The Significance of Research Objectives
Research objectives serve multiple critical functions in the research process. As highlighted by Saunders et al. (2019), well-crafted objectives:
"Establish the scope and depth of your project: This helps you avoid unnecessary research. It also means that your research methods and conclusions can easily be evaluated" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 1).
"Contribute to your research design: When you know what your objectives are, you have a clearer idea of what methods are most appropriate for your research" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 1). This connection between objectives and methodology is fundamental to research coherence (Maxwell, 2013).
"Indicate how your project will contribute to extant research: They allow you to display your knowledge of up-to-date research, employ or build on current research methods, and attempt to contribute to recent debates" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 1). This aspect is particularly important for situating your work within the scholarly conversation (Booth et al., 2016).
Developing Effective Research Aims and Objectives
The process of formulating research aims and objectives follows a logical progression that moves from general to specific. According to Punch (2016), this process typically involves three distinct steps:
Step 1: Formulating a General Aim
Begin by establishing broad research aim that encapsulates your research problem. As noted in the literature, your "research aim should reflect your research problem and should be relatively broad" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2). For example, an appropriate research aim might be "To assess the safety features and response times of self-driving cars" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2).
Step 2: Developing Specific Objectives
Once you've established your overarching aim, the next step involves breaking it down into specific, manageable components. Bell et al. (2018) recommends that researchers "break down your aim into a limited number of steps that will help you resolve your research problem" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2). Each objective should address a specific aspect of your research problem, such as "To measure the response time of self-driving cars to pedestrians" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2).
Step 3: Articulating Your Aims and Objectives Clearly
The final step involves clearly expressing your aims and objectives in your research document. Walliman (2017) emphasizes that "once you've established your research aim and objectives, you need to explain them clearly and concisely to the reader" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2). These statements typically appear at the conclusion of your problem statement within your introduction section.
When formulating these statements, use clear declarative language and appropriate verbs that accurately characterize the nature of your research activities (Leedy &amp; Ormrod, 2019). For example: "The aim of this research is to assess the safety features and response times of self-driving cars. Using quantitative methods, I will measure the response time of self-driving cars, first to other moving vehicles and then to pedestrians. I will also test their awareness of nearby inanimate obstacles (e.g., sidewalks)" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023).
The SMART Framework for Research Objectives
To ensure the quality and feasibility of research objectives, many scholars recommend using the SMART framework (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-based) (Sekaran &amp; Bougie, 2016). As detailed in research methodology literature:
Specific: "Make sure your objectives aren't overly vague. Your research needs to be clearly defined in order to get useful results" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2). Neuman (2014) notes that specificity helps maintain research focus.
Measurable: "Know how you'll measure whether your objectives have been achieved" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2). According to Creswell (2018), measurable objectives facilitate the evaluation of research outcomes.
Achievable: "Your objectives may be challenging, but they should be feasible. Make sure that relevant groundwork has been done on your topic or that relevant primary or secondary sources exist" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2). The feasibility assessment should also include consideration of available resources (Flick, 2018).
Relevant: "Make sure that they directly address the research problem you want to work on and that they contribute to the current state of research in your field" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2). This criterion ensures that your research contributes meaningfully to existing knowledge (Ravitch &amp; Carl, 2020).
Time-based: "Set clear deadlines for objectives to ensure that the project stays on track" (Academic Writing Resources, 2023, p. 2). According to Booth et al. (2016), time constraints help maintain research momentum and focus.
Research objectives represent a crucial component of the research process, providing structure, direction, and focus to your investigation. By carefully developing aims and objectives that follow the SMART criteria, researchers can ensure that their studies are well-designed, feasible, and contribute meaningfully to their field of study. Understanding the distinction between aims and objectives, as well as their respective roles in the research process, enables students to craft research proposals and projects that demonstrate scholarly rigor and methodological soundness.

Guidance: Provide a roadmap for selecting research methods and designing the study.

Types of Research Objectives
1. General Objectives
General objectives (also called broad objectives) provide an overview of the research’s primary aim. They describe the main purpose of the study in a concise manner.
Example: “To explore the relationship between exercise and mental health among young adults.”
2. Specific Objectives
Specific objectives break down the general objective into smaller, more focused goals. They address the research questions or hypotheses and guide data collection and analysis.
Example: 
“To assess the frequency of exercise among young adults.”
“To evaluate the impact of different types of exercise on anxiety levels.”
3. Descriptive Objectives
Descriptive objectives aim to describe characteristics, behaviors, or phenomena without investigating cause-and-effect relationships.
Example: “To describe the demographics of patients visiting a rural health clinic.”
4. Exploratory Objectives
Exploratory objectives seek to investigate unknown or poorly understood phenomena. They are often used in preliminary or qualitative research.
Example: “To explore the challenges faced by teachers transitioning to online education.”
5. Explanatory Objectives
Explanatory objectives aim to understand cause-and-effect relationships between variables.
Example: “To determine how income level influences consumer purchasing behavior.”
6. Evaluative Objectives
Evaluative objectives assess the effectiveness or outcomes of a specific program, intervention, or policy.
Example: “To evaluate the impact of a workplace wellness program on employee productivity.”

How to Write Effective Research Objectives
Step 1: Define Your Research Problem

1.5	Research Objective or Hypothesis
The formulation of research questions and hypotheses represents a fundamental step in the research process, providing both direction and structure to scholarly investigations (Bryman, 2016). The differences between these two research components are significant and understanding their distinctive characteristics is essential for developing methodologically sound research (Kumar, 2019). This comprehensive guide will explain the key differences between research questions and hypotheses with in-text citations, followed by a step-by-step approach for formulating each.
Research Questions vs. Research Hypotheses and Key Differences

Purpose
The purpose of a research question is distinctly different from that of a hypothesis. According to Creswell and Creswell (2018), research questions are designed "to explore, inquire, or understand a topic" without necessarily predicting specific outcomes. They serve as investigative tools that guide the overall research direction. In contrast, research hypotheses are formulated "to test a prediction or assumption" (Maxwell, 2013, p. 77), providing tentative explanations for observed phenomena that can be empirically tested.
Definition and Nature
Research questions are characterized by their open-ended, interrogative nature. As Flick (2018) explains, they serve as broad inquiries that guide the research process, allowing for exploration of phenomena without predetermining what might be found. Conversely, research hypotheses are "testable statements predicting the outcome" of investigations (Saunders et al., 2019, p. 144). Their declarative nature typically follows an "if...then..." structure or proposes specific relationships between variables.
Methodological Applications
The choice between research questions and hypotheses often aligns with specific methodological approaches. Research questions are predominantly "used in qualitative and exploratory studies" where the aim is to gain deeper insights into phenomena through rich descriptions and interpretations (Patton, 2015, p. 256). Hypotheses, however, are primarily "used in quantitative and experimental studies" that seek to establish causal relationships or correlations between variables through statistical analysis (Neuman, 2014, p. 203).
Testing Characteristics
An important distinction lies in the testability requirements of these research elements. Punch (2016) notes that research questions "may or may not be directly testable" as they can explore conceptual ideas that aren't immediately measurable. Hypotheses, by contrast, "must be testable and falsifiable" (Sekaran &amp; Bougie, 2016, p. 83), meaning they must make specific predictions that can be empirically verified or refuted through data collection and analysis.
Goals and Basis
The goals of research questions and hypotheses reflect their different functions in research. According to Bell et al. (2018), research questions aim "to identify variables and explore relationships" in a more open manner, while hypotheses seek "to confirm or refute a specific relationship" predicted in advance. Furthermore, as noted by Booth et al. (2016), research questions typically "arise from curiosity or gaps in literature," whereas hypotheses are more directly "derived from theory or prior research" that suggests specific relationships worth testing.
Formulating Research Questions: A Step-by-Step Guide

Step 1: Identify Your Research Interest and Problem
Begin by identifying a general area of interest and a specific research problem within that area. According to Leedy and Ormrod (2019), this initial step requires critical reflection on existing knowledge gaps and areas where further investigation would be valuable. Review relevant literature to understand what is already known about your topic and what remains unexplored (p. 43).
Step 2: Narrow Your Focus
After identifying your research problem, Ravitch and Carl (2020) recommend narrowing your focus to make your inquiry manageable. This involves identifying specific aspects of the broader topic that you want to investigate and ensuring that your scope is neither too broad nor too narrow (p. 76).
Step 3: Consider the FINER Criteria
When formulating research questions, apply the FINER criteria (Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, and Relevant). As Hulley et al. (2013) explain, "a good research question must be feasible to investigate with available resources, interesting to the researcher and community, novel in contributing new knowledge, ethical to conduct, and relevant to scientific knowledge or policy" (p. 17).
Step 4: Structure Your Question Appropriately
Research questions should be structured in clear, concise language. Creswell and Poth (2018) suggest that qualitative research questions often begin with words like "how" or "what" rather than "why," which allows for open exploration rather than causal determination (p. 138). For example: "How does sleep affect concentration in university students?"
Step 5: Ensure Alignment with Methodology
The final step involves ensuring that your research question aligns with your chosen methodology. According to Maxwell (2013), "your research questions should inform and be informed by your methodological choices" (p. 75). For instance, questions seeking to understand lived experiences are best suited for phenomenological approaches, while questions about social processes may be better addressed through grounded theory.
Formulating Research Hypotheses: A Step-by-Step Guide
Formulating research hypotheses involves a structured process grounded in existing knowledge. According to Bryman (2016), researchers must first review relevant literature, identify key variables, and articulate precise predictions about their relationships. These predictions must be testable and falsifiable (Neuman, 2014), typically expressed as both null and alternative hypotheses that can be empirically verified through appropriate statistical analysis (Sekaran &amp; Bougie, 2016).
Step 1: Review Relevant Theory and Research
The foundation for developing strong hypotheses lies in a thorough understanding of existing theory and research. Bryman (2016) emphasizes that hypotheses should emerge from "a critical review of theoretical frameworks and empirical findings" related to your research topic (p. 81). This theoretical grounding provides the rationale for your predictions.
Step 2: Identify Variables and Relationships
After reviewing the literature, identify the key variables involved in your research and specify the relationships you expect to find between them. Saunders et al. (2019) note that clearly defining your independent and dependent variables is essential for formulating testable hypotheses (p. 146).
Step 3: Formulate Clear Predictions
According to Kumar (2019), hypotheses should make clear, specific predictions about the expected relationships between variables (p. 132). These predictions should be stated in declarative form, such as: "Students who sleep less than 6 hours will have lower focus scores compared to those who sleep 7-8 hours."
Step 4: Ensure Testability and Falsifiability
Popper's principle of falsifiability is central to hypothesis development. As Neuman (2014) explains, "a good hypothesis must be potentially disprovable through empirical observation" (p. 204). This means formulating statements that can be tested with available measurement tools and that could potentially be shown to be false.
Step 5: State Both Null and Alternative Hypotheses
In quantitative research, it's standard practice to formulate both null (H₀) and alternative (H₁) hypotheses. According to Sekaran and Bougie (2016), "the null hypothesis typically states that no relationship exists between variables, while the alternative hypothesis proposes the specific relationship you expect to find" (p. 85). These paired hypotheses provide the framework for statistical testing.
The distinction between research questions and hypotheses reflects fundamental differences in research approaches and philosophical underpinnings. Research questions provide broad, exploratory frameworks ideal for qualitative and exploratory research, while hypotheses offer specific, testable predictions suited for quantitative and experimental designs (Creswell &amp; Creswell, 2018). By understanding these differences and following structured approaches to formulating both, researchers can develop coherent research designs that effectively address their investigative aims.

2.5	Conceptual Framework 
For a dissertation student, the conceptual framework is a critical component of the research process. It serves as a diagrammatic and narrative representation of how the researcher understands the relationships between the independent variables/concepts (factors influencing the phenomenon) and the dependent variable/concepts (the outcome or phenomenon being studied). It provides a structured way to visualize and explain the theoretical underpinnings of the study, guiding the research design and analysis.
Below is a detailed explanation of the conceptual framework and a step-by-step guide to developing it, tailored for a dissertation student.
What is a Conceptual Framework?
A conceptual framework is a visual and written explanation of the key variables or concepts in your study and how they are interrelated. It acts as a roadmap, showing how independent variables (e.g., factors like education level, income, or motivation) influence the dependent variable (e.g., academic performance, job satisfaction, or consumer behavior). It is grounded in theory and/or prior research, helping to justify the relationships you propose. The framework can be original (created by the student based on their understanding) or adopted/adapted from existing literature (with proper acknowledgment of the source).
Purpose of the Conceptual Framework
Clarifies Relationships: It shows how variables are expected to interact, providing clarity to the research problem.
Guides the Study: It helps in formulating research questions, hypotheses, and selecting appropriate methods.
Theoretical Foundation: It links the study to existing theories or concepts, ensuring academic rigor.
Focuses the Research: It keeps the study aligned with the key variables and avoids unnecessary complexity.

Components of a Conceptual Framework
Independent Variables/Concepts: These are the factors or variables that influence the outcome (e.g., training, leadership style, or technology adoption).
Dependent Variable/Concepts: This is the outcome or phenomenon being studied (e.g., employee performance, customer loyalty, or academic success).
Mediating/Moderating Variables (if applicable): These are variables that may influence the relationship between independent and dependent variables (e.g., age, organizational culture).
Arrows or Links: These indicate the direction and nature of relationships (e.g., positive, negative, direct, or indirect).
Narrative Explanation: A written description that explains the diagram and justifies the proposed relationships using theory or literature.
Steps to Develop a Conceptual Framework
Here is a structured, step-by-step process to create a robust conceptual framework for your dissertation:
Step 1: Identify the Research Problem and Key Variables
Clearly define the research problem and the objectives of your study.
Identify the dependent variable (the outcome you want to study) and the independent variables (the factors that influence the outcome).
Example: If studying factors affecting student academic performance, the dependent variable might be "academic performance," and independent variables could include "study habits," "teacher quality," and "access to resources."
Consider any mediating (intervening) or moderating (influencing the strength/direction) variables if relevant.
Step 2: Review Relevant Literature and Theories
Conduct a thorough literature review to identify theories, models, or prior studies relevant to your research topic.
Look for existing conceptual frameworks in similar studies that explain relationships between variables.
Identify theories that align with your study. For example: 
If studying motivation, you might use Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs or Self-Determination Theory.
If studying consumer behavior, you might use the Theory of Planned Behavior.
Note whether you will create your own framework or adopt/adapt an existing one from the literature.
Step 3: Develop or Adapt the Conceptual Framework
Option 1: Create Your Own Framework 
Based on your literature review, hypothesize how the independent variables influence the dependent variable.
Draw a diagram to represent these relationships: 
Place the dependent variable at the center or right side of the diagram.
Position the independent variables on the left or around it.
Use arrows to show the direction of influence (e.g., from independent to dependent variable).
Include mediating or moderating variables if applicable.
Ensure the framework is logical and supported by theory or prior studies.
Option 2: Adopt or Adapt an Existing Framework 
If using a framework from the literature, clearly cite the source (e.g., “This framework is adapted from Smith et al., 2020”).
Modify the framework if needed to fit your study’s context or variables.
Justify why the adopted framework is suitable for your research.

Step 4: Identify other influencing variables
It’s crucial to identify other variables that can influence the relationship between your independent and dependent variables early in your research process. Some common variables to include are moderating, mediating, and control variables.
Moderating variables
Moderating variables (or moderators) alter the effect that an independent variable has on a dependent variable. In other words, moderators change the “effect” component of the cause-and-effect relationship. Example: Moderator We expect that the number of hours a student studies is related to their exam score i.e., the more you prepare, the higher your score will be. Let’s add the moderator “IQ.” Here, a student’s IQ level can change the effect that the variable “hours of study” has on the exam score. The higher the IQ, the fewer hours of study are needed to do well on the exam. We expect that the “IQ” moderator moderates the effect that the number of study hours has on the exam score.
Mediating variables
Now we’ll expand the framework by adding a mediating variable. Mediating variables link the independent and dependent variables, allowing the relationship between them to be better explained (Swaen, 2024). Example: Mediator; The mediating variable of “number of practice problems completed” comes between the independent and dependent variables. Hours of study impacts the number of practice problems, which in turn impacts the exam score.
Control variables
Lastly, control variables must also be taken into account. These are variables that are held constant so that they don’t interfere with the results (Swaen, 2024). Even though you aren’t interested in measuring them for your study, it’s crucial to be aware of as many of them as you can be. Example: Control variable; It is very possible that if a student feels ill, they will get a lower score on the exam. However, we are not interested in measuring health outcomes a part of our research. This makes “health” a good candidate for a control variable. It still impacts our results, but we aren’t interested in studying it.
